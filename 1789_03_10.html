Thomas Beddoes to William Withering, 10 March 1789

Dear Sir,

I have not been inattentive amid a sufficient hurry of business to your commissions. I do not find that any medical person here has received any of the printed letters: & therefore I have followed your directions in keeping your statement by me. I hear but perhaps am misinformed that there has been another publication by D. on the subject.

I did not expect any success in my enquiries among the Oxford booksellers for Dillenius: & I am sorry to add that I have not been disappointed. It must be a rare accident that shd bring or detain any scarce & valuable work among them, their collections being confined to lecture-books & Greek Latin, & English Classicks.

Dr. Priestly has sent me his third paper & I am now constrained to relinquish the beautiful system of Lavoisier, at least the antiphlogistic part of it. He shewed me a curious phenomenon respecting the iron tube he uses for mift air & I sent him what I imagine to be the true explanation of it. I know not whether he has thought it worth imparting to the Society of Birmingham Philosophers.

The spirit of Chemistry has almost evaporated at Oxford, as indeed I always expected it wd . The young men are generally speaking regardless of every thing; & as there are few amusements here, they spend their time in the most stupid kind of dissipation imaginable; often sitting together & gaping at one another the whole day through. They find that when they have taken the degree of M.A. that they know little or nothing & then the better sort think it high time to bestir themselves. Accordingly my class was chiefly composed of the senior members of the University & still those who attend now for the third time make a considerable part of it. But their curiosity, as far as can be done in an elementary course, must be satisfied; & perhaps in half a dozen years more there will arise another race of men desirous to know something of this branch of Nat. Philosophy. At present, the stock of curiosity seems nearly exhausted.

Walker has frozen ☿ several times this winter; twice in my presence during the last snow. It is certainly not a perfect metal, having scarce more malleability than some of the semi-metals. It was each time frozen very hard, & a very small qty (3 drs perhaps) took 7 minutes in thawing. Yet no sooner had I flattened it a little than the edges began to crack & the whole mass soon broke into several pieces. I have not yet found time to draw up my paper. Dr Thomson thinks the specimens I shewed him exceedingly curious, & he agrees with me that they will tend very much to establish the igneous theory of the earth.

I am, Dr Sir, yours respectfully

Thomas Beddoes

10th March 1789

MS: Typed transcription inserted in a copy of Stock, Osler Library of the History of Medicine, McGill University Library, B399zs 1811
Address: Dr Withering / Birmingham
Endorsement: Beddoes Dr. 1789. / Chemistry. Approves Priestleys theory, / Science not encouraged in Oxford.
